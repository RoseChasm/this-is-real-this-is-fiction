When I was a kid my father never told the kind of work stories most parents did. "A client came in today who stapled his girlfriend to the wall," I remember him telling me while I did my spelling homework at the age of eight. Because my dad was a public defender in Philly, and that meant he saw the worst kind of violence and the most tragic injustice, by clients, lawyers and judges alike. One year of my life was colored by a particular case that had higher stakes than any other: his client's life. That year my father spent defending a man from the crime he almost certainly did do.

"God of Philadelphia" is a play based on the trial--One lawywer's attempt to save a man from the sex crime he was guilty of. It's a play that contains all the ambiguity of what it means to be fighting to save someone in a justice system too big for justice, an anti-To Kill a Mockingbird fit for a cynical age.
